The Importance of Affordable Housing


There is a growing recognition of the importance of affordable housing in ensuring economic stability and opportunity. A recent study by the National Low Income Housing Coalition found that a worker earning the minimum wage would need to work 99 hours per week to afford a modest two-bedroom rental home. This is an impossible task for most workers, particularly those who are also trying to raise a family.

The high cost of housing is not just a problem for low-wage workers. A report from the Joint Center for Housing Studies found that the number of cost-burdened households (those spending more than 30 percent of their income on housing) increased from 38.1 million in 2001 to 43.3 million in 2016. The problem is particularly acute for renters, with more than half of all renting households paying more than 30 percent of their income on housing.

The high cost of housing is a drag on the economy. Households that are spending a large share of their income on housing have less money to spend on other goods and services. This can lead to lower consumption and slower economic growth. In addition, the high cost of housing can lead to increased homelessness and housing instability, which can have negative consequences for health, employment, and educational outcomes.

The good news is that there is a growing recognition of the problem and a commitment to addressing it. In 2016, then-President Barack Obama established the White House Council on Hustle and Opportunity Zones to focus on revitalizing communities with high concentrations of poverty. And in 2018, Congress passed the Spending Bill, which included $20 billion for affordable housing programs.

The challenge now is to ensure that these commitments are translated into action. This will require a concerted effort from all levels of government, the private sector, and the nonprofit sector. We must work together to create more affordable housing options and to provide the support that families need to stay stably housed.
