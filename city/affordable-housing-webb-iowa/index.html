5 ways to make affordable housing work in your town


There's no one-size-fits-all answer to the affordable housing crisis, but there are some key things that communities can do to make affordable housing work for them. Here are five approaches that have shown promise:

1. Make it easier for people to build accessory dwelling units.

One way to increase the supply of affordable housing is to make it easier for people to build accessory dwelling units (ADUs) on their property. ADUs are small, secondary dwellings that can be built on a property that already has a primary residence. They can provide much-needed extra income for homeowners, as well as more affordable housing options for renters.

2. Incentivize homeowners to rent out rooms.

Another way to increase the supply of affordable housing is to incentivize homeowners to rent out rooms in their homes. This can be done through financial incentives, such as tax breaks, or by making it easier to obtain the necessary permits and licenses.

3. Encourage the development of co-housing.

Co-housing is a type of intentional community in which residents share common facilities and often share meals. This model can be an effective way to provide affordable housing, as it allows residents to pool their resources and minimize costs.

4. Implement inclusionary zoning.

Inclusionary zoning is a type of land-use regulation that requires developers to set aside a certain percentage of units in a new development for low- or moderate-income households. This can help to ensure that new development is accessible to a range of income levels.

5. Invest in publicly-owned land for affordable housing.

Another way to create more affordable housing is for municipalities to invest in publicly-owned land for the development of affordable housing. This can be done through direct investment or by partnering with nonprofit developers.

These are just a few of the many ways that communities can make affordable housing work for them. By taking action on one or more of these fronts, communities can begin to address the affordable housing crisis in a meaningful way.
