The lack of affordable housing is having a devastating effect on our economy and our society. People who can't afford to live in a decent place are more likely to have mental and physical health problems, difficulty finding and keeping a job, and problems with their kids' education. Whatever the means, it's essential that we take action soon to ensure that everyone has a place to call home.

The History of Affordable Housing


The term “affordable housing” is often used interchangeably with “subsidized housing.” However, there is a subtle but important distinction between the two. Affordable housing is housing that is affordable to low- and moderate-income households, while subsidized housing is housing that is provided with some form of government subsidy, such as a tax break or direct subsidy.

The concept of affordable housing has its roots in the 19th century, when a number of European countries began to experiment with ways to provide housing for the growing urban poor. One of the earliest and most successful examples was the German “sozialer wohnungsbau,” or social housing movement, which began in the late 19th century.

The social housing movement spread to other European countries, including the United Kingdom, France, and the Netherlands, in the early 20th century. In the United States, the first major effort to provide affordable housing was the New Deal’s Section 8 program, which was created in 1937.

The Section 8 program provided subsidies to private landlords who rented to low-income tenants. The program was expanded in the 1950s to include public housing projects, which were built and managed by local governments.

The public housing program was initially successful in providing affordable housing for low-income families. However, it eventually fell victim to mismanagement and corruption, and many public housing projects became rundown and dangerous.

The federal government began to wind down the public housing program in the 1970s, and the affordable housing crisis that we see today began to take shape. In the 1980s and 1990s, a number of private companies began to fill the void left by the public housing program, by developing and managing privately-owned affordable housing complexes.

Today, the affordable housing crisis is worse than ever, with millions of low- and moderate-income households struggling to find safe and affordable places to live. The good news is that there is a growing movement to address the affordable housing crisis, and a number of initiatives are underway to create more affordable housing.
