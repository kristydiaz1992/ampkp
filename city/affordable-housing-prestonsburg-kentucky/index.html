What is Affordable Housing and Who Does it Help?


Affordable housing is a type of housing that is designated as being affordable to low- and moderate-income households. In the United States, affordable housing is often provided by the government to help those who may not be able to afford a place to live otherwise.

Low-income households are defined as households that earn below 80% of the area median income (AMI), while moderate-income households are those that earn between 80-120% of the AMI.

There are many different types of affordable housing, such as public housing, Section 8 housing, and Low Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) properties.

Public housing is owned and operated by the government and is typically reserved for low-income households. Section 8 housing is a type of housing voucher program that helps low-income households pay for rent. LIHTC properties are privately-owned, but they must meet certain requirements in order to provide affordable housing.

Affordable housing is important because it helps to ensure that everyone has a place to live. It can also help to improve the quality of life for those who live in poverty by providing them with safe and affordable housing.

The History of Affordable Housing


The United States has a long history of affordable housing initiatives, starting with the New Deal programs of the 1930s. The goal of these programs was to provide housing for low- and moderate-income families, with the understanding that safe and decent housing is essential for health and well-being. The federal government continued to invest in affordable housing in the postwar period, with the creation of the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) and the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD).

The FHA provided mortgage insurance for private lenders, making it possible for more families to buy homes. HUD developed and continues to administer many programs that provide rental assistance for low-income families, seniors, and persons with disabilities. In the 1970s and 1980s, the federal government began to de-emphasize its role in affordable housing, and funding for HUD programs was cut back.

The private sector stepped in to fill the gap, with the development of low-income housing tax credit (LIHTC) program in the 1980s. The LIHTC program provides incentives for developers to build and operate affordable housing developments. In the 1990s and 2000s, there was a renewed interest in affordable housing at the federal level, with the passage of the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit expansion Act of 2000 and the Housing and Economic Recovery Act of 2008.

The current landscape of affordable housing includes a mix of private-sector developments and HUD-funded programs. There is a continued need for affordable housing in the United States, as evidenced by the high demand for assistance and the long waiting lists for many HUD programs. The future of affordable housing will likely involve a mix of public and private initiatives, with a continued focus on providing safe and decent housing for low- and moderate-income families.
