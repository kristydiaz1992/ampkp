What is Affordable Housing?



There is no one-size-fits-all answer to the question of what is affordable housing. The term generally refers to housing that is within the financial reach of low- and moderate-income households. But what is considered affordable varies widely from place to place, and even within individual communities.

In general, affordable housing is housing that does not cost more than 30 percent of a household’s monthly income. For example, a household with an income of $3,000 per month can afford to pay $900 per month for housing.

However, this general definition does not take into account the wide variation in housing costs from place to place. In some communities, housing prices are so high that even households with relatively high incomes cannot afford to buy a home or rent an apartment. In other communities, housing costs are lower, making it possible for households with modest incomes to afford a place to live.

The federal government has a number of programs to help low- and moderate-income households afford housing. These programs provide subsidies or direct financial assistance to help with rent or with the purchase of a home.

The most well-known of these programs is the Section 8 program, which provides subsidies to low-income households to help with rent. Other federal programs provide assistance for the purchase of a home, such as the Low-Income Housing Tax Credit program.

State and local governments also have programs to help with the cost of housing. For example, many states have programs that provide down payment assistance for first-time home buyers. Some local governments offer property tax breaks or other incentives to encourage the development of affordable housing.
