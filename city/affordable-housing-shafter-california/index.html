The Different Types of Affordable Housing


There are many types of affordable housing, each with its own unique benefits. Below, we will explore some of the most popular types of affordable housing so that you can decide which one is right for you.

1. Public Housing: Public housing is government-owned and -operated housing for low- and moderate-income families. Public housing is usually managed by state or local housing authorities. Benefits of public housing include affordability, as well as access to on-site amenities like laundry facilities, playgrounds, and community centers.

2. Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) Housing: LIHTC housing is privately owned and operated, but it must meet certain guidelines in order to be eligible for the tax credit. LIHTC housing is reserved for low- and moderate-income families, and it must be affordable. Like public housing, LIHTC housing often features on-site amenities.

3. Housing Choice Vouchers: Housing choice vouchers are a type of rent subsidy that can be used to help low- and moderate-income families afford housing in the private market. Families who receive housing vouchers can use them to help pay for rent, utilities, and other housing-related costs.

4. Section 8 Housing: Section 8 housing is a type of public housing that is specifically reserved for low-income families. Families who live in Section 8 housing receive a subsidy that helps them pay for rent. Section 8 housing is often managed by state or local housing authorities.

5. Manufactured Housing: Manufactured housing refers to homes that are built in a factory and then transported to a site. Manufactured homes can be a more affordable option for families, and they can be placed in a variety of settings, including mobile home parks, RV parks, and on private land.

6. Cooperative Housing: Cooperative housing is a type of housing in which residents own shares in the property. Cooperative housing can be a more affordable option for families, and it often includes amenities like playgrounds, pools, and community rooms.

7. Senior Housing: Senior housing is housing that is specifically designed for older adults. Senior housing can include independent living, assisted living, and nursing home care. Senior housing often includes amenities like meals, transportation, and social activities.

8. Student Housing: Student housing is housing that is specifically designed for college students. Student housing can be a more affordable option for students, and it often includes amenities like laundry facilities, study areas, and recreation rooms.

9. Low-Cost Housing: Low-cost housing is housing that is affordable for low- and moderate-income families. Low-cost housing can be found in a variety of settings, including public housing, LIHTC housing, and Section 8 housing.

10. Housing for People with Disabilities: Housing for people with disabilities is housing that is specifically designed to meet the needs of people with disabilities. Housing for people with disabilities can include independent living, assisted living, and nursing home care. Housing for people with disabilities often includes amenities like accessible kitchens and bathrooms, and expanded doorways and hallways.

The History of Affordable Housing in America


The first government-subsidized housing program in America was the New Deal’s Federal Housing Administration (FHA), which was created in 1934. The FHA’s mission was to encourage home ownership by insuring private lenders against loss if borrowers defaulted on their mortgages. The FHA helped to fuel America’s post-World War II housing boom by making it easier for families to obtain loans for new homes.

During the 1950s and 1960s, the federal government continued to play a role in the housing market by providing subsidies for the construction of low-income housing. In 1965, the federal government created the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) to administer housing programs. HUD’s first director, Robert C. Weaver, was a strong advocate for affordable housing. Under Weaver’s leadership, HUD created several programs to increase the supply of affordable housing, including the Section 8 program, which provides subsidies to private landlords who agree to rent to low-income tenants.

Since the 1970s, the federal government’s role in the housing market has declined. HUD’s budget has been cut repeatedly and many of its programs have been eliminated or scaled back. As a result, the availability of affordable housing has decreased. The number of homeless people in America has increased, and the problem of affordable housing has become increasingly dire.

The federal government’s role in the housing market is likely to increase in the coming years as the new administration has proposed a $1.5 trillion investment in infrastructure, which includes $200 billion for affordable housing. It remains to be seen whether these proposed investments will be enough to address the country’s affordable housing crisis.
