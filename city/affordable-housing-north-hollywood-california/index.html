California's Proposition 10 and the future of affordable housing


The California Legislature is considering a ballot measure that would repeal a state law limiting the ability of cities and counties to enact rent control. The measure, known as Proposition 10, would have a profound impact on California's housing market, and could lead to a significant increase in the construction of affordable housing.

The state law in question, the Costa-Hawkins Rental Housing Act, was enacted in 1995 and limits the ability of cities and counties to enact rent control. Under Costa-Hawkins, rent control can only be applied to buildings constructed before 1995, and cannot be applied to single-family homes or condos.

Proposition 10 would repeal Costa-Hawkins, and would allow cities and counties to enact rent control on any type of housing. This would have a significant impact on the supply of affordable housing in California, as rent control would make it more difficult for landlords to profit from rent increases.

The impact of Proposition 10 on the construction of new housing is less clear. Some argue that repealing Costa-Hawkins would lead to more construction of new housing, as developers would be able to build rent-controlled units and charge below-market rents. Others argue that repealing Costa-Hawkins would have the opposite effect, as developers would be less likely to build new housing if they couldn't charge market-rate rents.

There is no easy answer to the question of whether Proposition 10 would lead to more or less affordable housing in California. However, the measure would undoubtedly have a major impact on the state's housing market, and could lead to significant changes in the way that housing is priced and regulated.
