The History of Affordable Housing in South Boston


The history of affordable housing in South Boston is a long and complicated one. The area has been home to waves of immigrants and working-class families for centuries, and the housing stock has always been a mix of tenement buildings, triple-deckers, and single-family homes. In the early 20th century, the construction of the South Boston waterfront created a boom in the construction of new homes and apartments, many of which were rented to working-class families.

However, the Great Depression and the post-World War II housing shortage caused a sharp decrease in the availability of affordable housing in South Boston. In the 1950s and 1960s, the city began demolishing old tenement buildings and erecting public housing projects in their place. While these projects did provide much-needed housing for low-income families, they also changed the character of the neighborhood.

In the 1980s and 1990s, gentrification began to take hold in South Boston. Young professionals and artists were attracted to the area by its affordability and proximity to downtown. This influx of new residents led to the renovation of many old buildings and the construction of new luxury condos and apartments. As a result, the availability of affordable housing in South Boston has decreased dramatically in recent years.

Despite these challenges, there are still a number of organizations and programs working to ensure that low- and moderate-income families have access to safe, decent, and affordable housing in South Boston. These efforts are essential to preserving the diversity and character of the neighborhood.
